# Concept to Metaphor

## Core principle

A good metaphor preserves the structure of the concept.

It should answer:

```text
What corresponds to what?
What relationship is preserved?
What part of the metaphor is only approximate?
```

## Structure mapping

Before finalizing a metaphor, write a quick mapping:

```text
Concept element A → Visual element A
Concept element B → Visual element B
Concept relationship → Visual action
Concept limitation → Visual caution
```

Example:

```text
Opportunity cost:
choice → opening one door
alternatives → other doors
best foregone alternative → the brightest fading door
not all alternatives → do not combine all doors into one giant cost
```

## Strong metaphor examples

### Opportunity cost

```text
A person chooses one door while the best unchosen door fades.
```

Why it works:

```text
It shows choice and sacrifice.
```

Risk:

```text
Do not show other doors destroyed forever.
```

### Entropy

```text
A tidy room becomes messy unless energy is spent cleaning it.
```

Why it works:

```text
It shows that order requires maintenance.
```

Risk:

```text
Do not reduce entropy to “garbage.”
```

### RAG

```text
A helper searches a library for evidence cards before answering.
```

Why it works:

```text
It shows retrieval before generation.
```

Risk:

```text
Do not imply the answer is always correct.
```

### Natural selection

```text
Camouflaged beetles survive more often and appear more in the next generation.
```

Why it works:

```text
It shows variation, environment, survival, and inheritance.
```

Risk:

```text
Do not show animals deciding to evolve.
```

## Bad metaphor warning

Avoid metaphors that:

- only look cool
- make the concept wrong
- add false agency
- hide the main relationship
- require more explanation than the original concept
- turn neutral concepts into moral stories
